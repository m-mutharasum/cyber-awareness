THE PASSWORD CRISIS

"12345" IS DEAD: THE RISE OF PASSPHRASES

FIG 2.1: ENTROPY ANALYSIS

Complexity is out. Length is in. Security experts at CISA now recommend Passphrases over complex passwords. A string like Horse-Battery-Staple-Correct is mathematically harder to crack than P@ssw0rd1 and easier to remember.

FIG 2.2: Demo on - Why strong password?

The 16-Character Standard: Brute force algorithms can crack an 8-character password in minutes. A 16-character phrase takes billions of years.

THE SECOND LOCK

MULTI-FACTOR DEFENSE

FIG 2.3: Demo on - What is MFA?

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) adds a critical layer. It combines something you know (Password) with something you have (OTP/Phone). This stops 99.9% of automated attacks.

FIG 2.4: BIOMETRIC AUTH
BEST PRACTICES
  • Never reuse passwords.
  • Use a Password Manager.
  • Change immediately if leaked.

SOCIAL ENGINEERING

DON'T CLICK THAT LINK: ANATOMY OF A SCAM

Hackers don't hack computers; they hack minds. Social Engineering uses fear, urgency, and greed to trick users.

FIG 3.1: PHISHING RED FLAGS

Phishing emails often claim your "Account is Suspended" to panic you into clicking. Always check the sender's address (e.g., support@paypa1.com vs paypal.com).

FIG 3.2: DEMO on PHISHING ATTACK
FIG 3.3: PHISHING DIFFERENCE

New Threat: Quishing. Cybercriminals are pasting fake QR code stickers on parking meters and restaurant menus. These codes bypass email filters and deliver malware directly to mobile devices.

FIG 3.4: QUISHING PHISHING

Defense Protocol: Hover over links before clicking. Verify requests for money via a separate phone call.

FROM THE ARCHIVES: REAL WORLD FAILURES

FIG 5.1: MFA FATIGUE ATTACK

CASE 01: UBER

The MFA Fatigue Attack

Incident: In 2022, a hacker spammed an Uber employee with login notifications at 2 AM. Annoyed, the employee finally clicked "Approve" to stop the noise.

FIG 5.2: NOTIFICATION SPAM

Lesson: Never approve a login request you didn't initiate.

FIG 5.3: GLOBAL INFECTION SPREAD

CASE 02: WANNACRY

The Patching Failure

Incident: In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware infected 200,000 computers in 150 countries, crippling the UK's NHS. It exploited a Windows vulnerability that had a patch available months prior.

FIG 5.4: CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE HIT

Lesson: Update software immediately. Patches close security holes.
